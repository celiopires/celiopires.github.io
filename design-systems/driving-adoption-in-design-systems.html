Driving adoption in design systems

Building a design system is one thing. Getting people to actually use it is another. Across projects like El Español, Telefónica, Commons, and Metallicus I saw the same pattern: great components, zero adoption, and chaos creeping back in.

Design Systems - Driving Adoption

Why adoption matters

Without adoption:

• teams build their own versions
• logic shifts from squad to squad
• engineers patch things on the fly
• reviews drag on
• design debt piles up

A system only works if people use it. Otherwise it's just a Figma file.

My role

I didn't just build components. I made teams understand why the system mattered, how to use it, and how to keep it alive. Half my time was talking, half was designing.

How I made it stick

Getting teams to actually use the system required a mix of strategy, communication, and persistence.

Getting buy-in

Show the cost of chaos: slow delivery, mismatched UI, more bugs. People adopt when they see the pain.

Engineering alignment

Loop in developers early, agree on naming and logic. Even without Storybook, the system mapped cleanly to code.

Rollout

Small teams: library + guide. Large teams: training, migration plan, feedback loop.

Handling resistance

"This doesn't fit my flow?" I reviewed, adjusted, or explained trade-offs. Most pushback is clarity, not intent.

Keeping it alive

Review new components, clean duplicates, audit usage, refactor when the product shifts. Simple, consistent care.

Impact

Teams shipped faster, UI mismatches dropped, less rework, cleaner code, happier designers and engineers.

Sometimes bug counts went down.

Sometimes the same buttons stopped being rebuilt three times a week.

Sometimes both.

What I learned

A design system isn't just components.

It's alignment, trust, and adoption.

The structure matters, but adoption is what makes a system real.
